# Slide Design Principles

Principles for creating effective slides and presentation visuals. Drawn from Presentation Zen and Universal Principles of Design. Use these when generating any slide-based output.

---

## The Core Mindset

**A slide is visual support for a human voice, not a document for storage.**

If the audience is reading, the speaker is failing. Every slide must be processed *visually* — in under 3 seconds — while the voice carries the narrative. The moment a slide demands reading, it has become a competitor to the speaker rather than a support.

### Slides for You vs. Slides for Others

Not all slide decks are presented. Before designing, ask: **who is this for?**

| Audience | Design direction |
|---|---|
| **Personal reference** (your own notes, thinking aid) | Dense is fine. Bullet lists are fine. Ugly is fine. You're the only reader — optimize for *your* recall, not for visual polish. |
| **Presented to peers** (team review, technical walkthrough) | Moderate simplification. They share your context, so you can go deeper. But still: one idea per slide, headlines that state the point. |
| **Presented to non-technical audience** (clients, leadership, public) | Maximum subtraction. No jargon. Visuals over text. Story over data. Every slide must pass the 3-second test. |
| **Sent as a document** (emailed deck, async review) | This is a **slideument** — the anti-pattern. If it will be read without a speaker, make a proper document instead. Slides without a voice are just bad documents. |

**The rule**: The less you will be in the room to explain, the more each slide must be self-contained. But the more self-contained a slide becomes, the less it is a *slide* and the more it is a *document*. If you can't present it live, don't call it a slide deck.

## 1. Subtraction Over Addition (Kanso)

The primary design move is *removal*, not addition.

- **If an element can be removed without losing the message, it must go.**
- Every element on a slide increases cognitive load. Each one must earn its place.
- Negative space is not wasted space — it signals clarity, confidence, and luxury. Clutter signals anxiety and low status.
- Apply the **Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) test**: remove any element that does not directly support the message.

**Do:**
- One idea per slide
- Generous margins and breathing room
- Remove logos from all slides except first and last (Logo Audit)

**Don't:**
- Decorative borders, stock clipart, or "icing" added at the end
- Multiple competing ideas on one slide
- Filling empty space because it "feels empty"

---

## 2. Paper First, Then Digital

Software is a container for ideas, not a generator. The formatting temptation of PowerPoint leads to structural bloat.

- **The Paper First Rule**: When overwhelmed by content, close the computer. Use Post-it notes and a Sharpie to structure ideas *before* touching slides.
- If you cannot structure the idea on a Post-it note, you are not ready to put it on a slide.
- Sketch the visual on paper first before importing images. Forces understanding of structure before rendering.

**The test**: *"Can I explain this slide's purpose in one sentence while pointing at it?"* If no, it's not ready.

---

## 3. Cognitive Load Management

The brain cannot read and listen simultaneously. These are competing channels.

- **Replace text bullets with visuals 80% of the time.** Icons, diagrams, photographs — anything processed visually rather than verbally.
- **The Inverted Pyramid**: Lead with the conclusion (What/Why), then support. Never build to a reveal — the audience needs the frame before the detail.
- **The "So What?" Audit (Dakara Nani?)**: For every piece of content, ask: *"Is this relevant to the core story, or merely impressive?"* If impressive but irrelevant, cut it.
- **Chunk ruthlessly**: Working memory holds 4±1 items. If a slide has more than 5 elements, it's overloaded.
- **First and last matter most**: The brain weights beginnings and ends higher than middles (Serial Position Effect). Your opening slides and closing slides are the most remembered. Your strongest point should be first, your call-to-action should be last. The middle is where attention fatigues — keep it lean.

**Do:**
- Headlines that state the slide's point (not topic labels like "Q3 Results")
- Single powerful image with minimal text
- Diagrams over bullet lists

**Don't**
- Paragraphs of text on slides
- Bullet lists longer than 5 items
- Slides that require the audience to "figure out" the point

## 4. The Big Four — Visual Architecture

Use these four principles to arrange every slide:

| Principle | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Contrast** | Creates visual hierarchy and draws attention | Large headline vs. small caption; bold color vs. muted |
| **Repetition** | Creates consistency and professionalism | Same font, same color accent, same layout pattern across slides |
| **Alignment** | Creates order and connection between elements | Everything on an invisible grid; no floating elements |
| **Proximity** | Groups related items, separates unrelated | Related data points close together; section breaks with whitespace |

If a slide feels "off," check these four. Usually alignment or proximity is broken.

---

## 5. Story Over Data

Facts are available online. Stories are how humans process them.

- The brain retains narrative and conflict for survival, not isolated data.
- Logic is the baseline; story and emotion are the differentiators for impact.
- **Structure as narrative**: Setup → Conflict → Resolution. Not: Topic → Subtopic → Detail.
- Every presentation answers one question. Find it. State it. Everything else supports it.

**The "Bento Lens"**: Content must be balanced and purposeful. If a slide lacks purpose or balance, it is "uncooked" and distracts from the core message.

---

## 6. Barrier Removal

Barriers exist in three layers. Audit all three before delivery:

| Layer | Examples | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical** | Lectern hiding body, dim room hiding face, fixed seating | Walk the space before; remove barriers; ensure lighting on face |
| **Technological** | Tethered remote, clicking through laptop, slide transitions | Wireless presenter remote; rehearse transitions; use "B" key to go black |
| **Cognitive** | Jargon, clutter, dense text, complex diagrams | Simplify language; reduce elements; one idea per slide |

**The "B" Key Reset**: If the audience is drifting, hit "B" to black the screen, or stand up and move. Resets attention through novelty and embodied presence.

---

## 7. Presence Protocol

The speaker is the primary medium. Technical perfection is secondary to human connection.

- **The No-Apology Protocol**: When nervous, suppress the urge to verbalize it. Apologies shift focus to internal state, breaking the connection frame.
- **Eye-light connection**: The audience must see the presenter's face and eyes. A dimmed room breaks this.
- **"Mushin" (no mind)**: Focus on the audience, not the performance. If you're thinking about your slides, you've lost them.

## Anti-Patterns (What to Reject)

| Trap | Red Flag | Why it fails |
|---|---|---|
| **The Slideument** | Dense text on slides; printing slides as handouts | Sacrifices clarity for speed; turns presentation into reading assignment |
| **The Template Trap** | Using stock PowerPoint templates | Creates visual fatigue; signals amateur; templates hold assets, not ideas |
| **Expert Blindness** | Believing data alone moves people | Experts see fewer possibilities than beginners; data without story is noise |
| **Decoration vs. Design** | Adding "icing" at the end | Design starts at the beginning (preparation); decoration is a band-aid |
| **The Steve Jobs Copy** | Mimicking style without substance | Jobs worked because of authenticity, not turtleneck; copying kills principle |
| **More = Better** | Adding more visuals/slides for "comprehensiveness" | Every visual adds cognitive load; ask if it replaces words or decorates them |

## Quick Decision Guide

```
Building a slide deck?
│
├── Haven't structured ideas yet?
│   └── → Paper first. Post-its + Sharpie. No software.
│
├── Slide has more than 5 elements?
│   └── → Subtract. Remove until message breaks, then add back one.
│
├── Slide has paragraphs of text?
│   └── → Convert to visual (diagram, icon, image) or move to handout.
│
├── Bullet list longer than 5 items?
│   └── → Split into multiple slides or convert to visual hierarchy.
│
├── Slide title is a topic label ("Q3 Results")?
│   └── → Rewrite as a claim ("Revenue grew 23% driven by APAC expansion").
│
├── Audience is reading, not listening?
│   └── → Too much text. Replace with image + headline. Speaker carries detail.
│
└── Slide feels cluttered but everything is "important"?
    └── → Apply SNR test. If removing it doesn't break the story, cut it.
```

## Tensions & Limits

- **Simplicity vs. Accuracy**: In highly technical fields (legal, medical, engineering), the simplicity rule risks oversimplifying. Move complex data to handouts; keep slides for the story.
- **Western Bias**: These principles assume a Western, linear reading eye and a presenter with high emotional intelligence. Adapt for cultural context.
- **Formal Constraints**: Courtrooms, boardrooms, and formal settings may prohibit physical freedom. Adapt to the constraint rather than rejecting the system.
- **Emotional Context**: In high-emotion situations (crisis, grief), the brain prioritizes survival over efficiency. Cost-benefit logic of design breaks down.
